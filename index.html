NTC-IQ

Bridging the Gap Between Network Intelligence and AI

NTC-IQ is an academic initiative aimed at enabling network-aware artificial intelligence. We present a research-driven framework to analyze and classify encrypted network traffic using RFC-compliant features—ensuring privacy preservation without sacrificing performance.

With a series of academic publications, NTC-IQ:

  • Evaluates contemporary machine learning approaches for network traffic classification and reveals the systemic consequences of using outdated, pre-TLS 1.3 datasets.
  • Identifies and addresses deeply rooted methodological flaws that undermine academic studies, commercial products, and patented solutions.
  • Challenges the status quo of increasing computational complexity and the widespread neglect of real-world protocol behaviors.
  • Demonstrates that minimal, RFC-aligned representations can achieve state-of-the-art results while being dramatically more efficient.

Our Research

1. SoK: Decoding the Enigma of Encrypted Network Traffic Classifiers

The study provides a critical evaluation of machine learning (ML)-based network traffic classification (NTC) in the era of pervasive encryption (e.g., TLS 1.3). It identifies major shortcomings in current NTC research, including the widespread use of outdated and unencrypted datasets, poor design decisions leading to inflated accuracies, and reliance on unsubstantiated assumptions.

Key contributions include:

  1. Taxonomy of NTC Literature: provides a structured analysis of ML-based NTC methods, focusing on design and benchmarking choices.
  2. Dataset Evaluation: demonstrates that many widely used datasets contain mostly unencrypted traffic or rely on deprecated cipher suites, making them unfit for evaluating modern NTC systems.
  3. CipherSpectrum Dataset: introduces a new dataset with traffic encrypted using all three cipher suites mandated/strongly-recommended by TLS 1.3.
  4. Empirical Critique: exposes how design choice oversights lead to overfitting and poor generalization using 348 occlusion experiments.
  5. Recommendations: issues guidelines for avoiding overfitting and building generalizable classifiers.

Overall, the work offers a systematic critique of the field, highlighting methodological flaws and proposing best practices for future NTC research.

2. Less is More: Simplifying Network Traffic Classification Leveraging RFCs

The LiM study introduces a lightweight, RFC-compliant methodology for encrypted network traffic classification (NTC). The study critiques prevailing deep-learning-based approaches for relying on flawed assumptions and inefficient data representations, proposing a minimalist yet effective alternative.

Key contributions include:

  • Problem Analysis: The paper identifies five critical issues in current NTC methods:
    • C1: Assumes exploitable patterns exist in encrypted payloads, which contradicts TLS 1.3's encryption guarantees.
    • C2: Uses fixed-size payload representations that obscure valuable traffic characteristics.
    • C3: Includes session-specific, noisy header fields that do not generalize well.
    • C4: Converts network traffic into text or images, distorting protocol semantics.
    • C5: Depends on handshake packets that are often absent due to session reuse and 0-RTT in TLS 1.3.
  • NetMatrix Representation: A compact tabular format using three RFC-compliant attributes:
    • Total Length (IP header)
    • Time-To-Live (TTL)
    • Inter-Arrival Time
    This representation requires only 30 bytes per session, significantly reducing data volume compared to deep learning models.
  • LiM Classifier: A simple XGBoost-based classifier paired with NetMatrix that rivals state-of-the-art deep learning models in performance without the computational overhead.
  • Experimental Validation: Evaluated on the CSTNET-TLS1.3 dataset, LiM:
    • Achieves 94.2% accuracy, close to YaTC (96.3%) and surpassing ET-BERT (56.8%).
    • Drastically reduces latency, memory, and energy usage.
    • Achieves over 86,000 inferences/second, making it ideal for real-time use cases.
  • Practical Implications: Demonstrates that RFC-aligned, resource-efficient methods like LiM are viable alternatives to complex deep-learning-based NTC approaches.

The paper advocates for simplicity in NTC through RFC-guided design. By rejecting complex transformations and focusing on meaningful, stable features, LiM demonstrates that efficiency and effectiveness are not mutually exclusive.
